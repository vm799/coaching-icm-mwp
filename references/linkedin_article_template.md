# LinkedIn Article Template — Enterprise Thought Leadership

Stage 04 produces LinkedIn article from enterprise script.
Different from blog post: shorter, punchy, executive-facing, personal voice.

---

# [Topic] — LinkedIn Article

## Article Metadata
- **Target length:** 800-1500 words (1000 sweet spot)
- **Tone:** Personal authority. First person. "I've seen this..." not "Research shows..."
- **Audience:** C-suite, product leaders, tech leads, board-level execs
- **Goal:** Establish thought leadership. Drive to video / blog / consultation.

---

## Structure

### Headline (One line, under 100 chars)
Pattern: [Bold claim] — [Why it matters to executive]

Examples:
- "Why Most AI Projects Fail (It's Not the Model)"
- "Safe AI Isn't Slower AI. Here's What That Means for Your Business."
- "Tokens Are Attention. Here's How We Stop Wasting Them."

**Rules:**
- Specific, not vague ("40% cost reduction" > "better performance")
- Controversy or counterintuitive claim performs better than neutral
- Test: would a CFO or CTO share this with their team?

---

### Opening Hook (1-2 sentences)
Bold claim. Immediate.

> "Most AI deployments I see have the same problem. They're optimizing for the wrong thing."

OR

> "I've advised 12 enterprise AI teams in the last 18 months. The failure pattern is identical every time."

**Rule:** No warm-up. LinkedIn readers skim. First 2 lines = stay or leave.

---

### Problem Setup (100-200 words)
What's the problem? Why does it cost them?

Write in first person: "What I see is..." / "The teams I work with struggle with..."
Include one data point or pattern-from-experience.

> "The problem isn't the model. It's the context. Most teams load everything they have into the prompt — every document, every conversation, every instruction — hoping the AI figures out what matters. It doesn't. It costs money, slows responses, and reduces accuracy. I call this 'context hoarding.' It's the enterprise AI equivalent of handing someone a library card and saying 'go find the answer yourself.'"

---

### Core Insight (150-250 words)
The insight that makes this article worth reading.

Use personal credibility: "In my experience..." / "When I built X..."
Use analogy: connects complex concept to familiar executive experience.

---

### Evidence (100-200 words)
Data, case study, or measured result. Short. Specific.

> "We ran a test. Before selective context: 4,200 tokens per request. After: 1,800. Same accuracy. Cost dropped 57%. The bottleneck was never the model. It was the context."

If no internal data: use industry benchmark + attribute it.

---

### Application (200-300 words)
What do THEY do? Executive-level steps, not technical instructions.

Pattern:
> "Here's what this means for your team:
> 
> 1. Audit your current prompt templates. What's in there that isn't needed?
> 2. Define context budgets per use case.
> 3. Measure before and after. Token count + accuracy.
> 
> This takes one sprint. The savings compound every month."

---

### Soundbite Drop (1-2 sentences)
Quotable. Standalone. The thing they'll screenshot and share.

> "'Tokens are attention. Attention is expensive. This is how we spend them wisely.' If that's the only thing your team takes from this, you've already saved money."

---

### Closing (50-100 words)
CTA. Specific. One action.

Options:
- "I cover this in full in my latest video: [link]. 30 minutes."
- "If you're navigating this in your organisation, book 30 minutes. Happy to walk you through our approach."
- "Follow for weekly breakdowns on enterprise AI implementation."

**Rule:** One CTA only. Don't give them 4 options.

---

## Writing Style

| Use | Avoid |
|-----|-------|
| "I've seen this pattern 12 times" | "Research shows..." |
| Short paragraphs (2-3 lines) | Dense blocks of text |
| Bullet lists for steps/data | Inline list of 6+ items |
| Bold for key lines | Italics for everything |
| Direct ("This costs you X") | Hedged ("This might potentially...") |
| First person | Third person or passive voice |
| One idea per paragraph | Two ideas fighting in one paragraph |

---

## LinkedIn-Specific Formatting

**Show More truncation:** First 3 lines determine whether they click "see more." Hook = first 3 lines.

**Line breaks:** Leave blank line between every paragraph (LinkedIn renders tightly without line breaks).

**Bold:** Use for 2-3 key lines per article. Not entire sections.

**Lists:** Short. Max 5 items. Each item 1-2 lines.

**Images:** Add 1 image (diagram, screenshot, or chart). Images get 2x reach on LinkedIn.

**No markdown:** LinkedIn doesn't render `#headers` or `**bold**` in articles. Use platform formatting tools.

## Article vs Post (What's The Difference)

| Article (long-form) | Post (short-form) |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| 800-1500 words | 100-300 words |
| Published as LinkedIn Article | Published as status update |
| SEO-indexable | Feed only |
| Deeper thought leadership | Snackable insight |
| Links OK throughout | Links in comment (not post) |
| Use for: quarterly cornerstone topic | Use for: daily/weekly reaction or soundbite |

**When to write article vs post:**
- New video published → Article (long-form breakdown) + Post (clip + teaser)
- Speaking at event → Post (announcement) + Post-event (story + photo)
- Key insight → Post (soundbite) → if engagement good → expand to Article

---

## Posting Checklist

- [ ] Headline: specific, bold, under 100 chars
- [ ] First 3 lines: hook without warm-up
- [ ] One soundbite / quotable moment
- [ ] One data point or case study
- [ ] One clear CTA (not four)
- [ ] Image added (diagram, screenshot, or chart)
- [ ] No jargon without definition
- [ ] Read aloud test: would an exec say "I need to share this with my team"?

---

## Post-Publish Engagement (First Hour)

LinkedIn rewards early engagement. In first hour after posting:
- Reply to every comment
- Like every comment
- Tag 2-3 people who'd find it genuinely useful (not spam-tagging)
- If a comment adds value: "Great point — [expand on it in reply]"

First-hour engagement signals quality to algorithm → wider reach.
