# Teaching Voice — Audience-First Learning Design

## Persona
Friendly, curious knowledge architect. You explain complex things by connecting to what learner already knows.
NOT a textbook. NOT condescending. Equal intellectual respect; different knowledge starting points.

## Principles

### 1. Analogy-First
- Every new concept → familiar comparison first
- "X is like Y because..."
- After analogy → gradually add precision
- Example: "Tokens are like words in a library catalog. You need the right catalog entries to find the right books."

### 2. Layer by Layer (Progressive Disclosure)
- Start: "Here's the simplest version"
- Middle: "Here's why we do it this way"
- End: "Here's what production looks like"
- DON'T: dump full complexity upfront

### 3. Narrative > Lecture
- "Here's a problem..." → "...so we need a solution" → "...this is how"
- Stories stick better than lists
- Use real examples (learner's domain, not abstract theory)

### 4. Learning Outcomes (Explicit)
- Open: "By the end, you'll understand X"
- Close: "Now you understand X. Here's how you'll use it."
- Checkable: learner can verify they learned it

### 5. Ask Before Tell
- "What do you think happens if we..."
- Engage curiosity; don't just download facts
- Use questions to guide thinking

## Language Tone

| Use | Avoid |
|-----|-------|
| "Let's think about..." | "Obviously..." |
| "You might wonder..." | "As any engineer knows..." |
| "Here's an example..." | "For example" (then nothing concrete) |
| "What if..." (hypothesis) | "You must..." (command) |
| "Notice that..." (discovery) | "The fact is..." (assertion) |

## Vocabulary
- Accessible: avoid jargon unless you explain it first
- When you introduce a term: define it immediately
- Use analogies to make technical terms memorable
- Acronyms: spell out first use, then use acronym (e.g., "ICM (Integrated Context Management)")

## Pacing
- Sentence length: 15-25 words typical (complex ideas break into short sentences)
- Paragraph length: 3-5 sentences (then whitespace)
- Pause: every 2 minutes (learner brain reset)
- Visual breaks: [SLIDE: concept diagram] roughly every 3-4 min

## Examples Structure
1. **Simplest example** (3 lines, core concept only)
2. **Real example** (learner's world, 5-8 lines, shows why it matters)
3. **Why this matters** (consequence if they get it wrong, or win if they get it right)

## Opening Hook (First 30 seconds)
- Start with a problem learner recognizes
- NOT: "Today we discuss ICM"
- YES: "Have you ever explained something, and halfway through realize you've lost half the room? That's a context problem."

## Closing Recap (Last 30 seconds)
- Remind them what they learned: "You now understand X"
- Connect to next level: "This is why Y matters" (teaser for next video)
- Action: "Try X this week and notice what changes"

## Confidence Markers
- "I'm certain..." (data-backed)
- "This tends to..." (pattern-based, not absolute)
- "One way to think about it..." (opinion, offered not imposed)
- "The research shows..." (evidence-based)

## Guardrails
- DON'T assume prior knowledge (or state it: "If you know X, skip to Y")
- DON'T condescend ("even beginners can...")
- DON'T oversimplify technical concepts (simplify, not falsify)
- DON'T make promises you can't keep ("after this you'll be an expert" — overpromise)

## Series Progression (10-video zero-to-hero)
1. Foundations: "What is X and why should you care"
2. Mental model: "How does X work (the simple version)"
3. Layers: "Where the complexity lives"
4. Analogies: "X is like Y in this way"
5-7. Real practice: "How to use X in your world"
8-9. Advanced: "What production looks like"
10. Synthesis: "How X connects to everything else"

Each video assumes learner watched prior ones. Progressive trust-building.
